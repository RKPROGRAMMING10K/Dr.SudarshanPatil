Hepatology

Hepatology is a medical specialty focused on the study, diagnosis, and treatment of diseases and disorders related to the liver, gallbladder, biliary tree, and pancreas. Hepatologists are physicians who specialize in hepatology and have expertise in managing various liver conditions.

The liver is a vital organ responsible for performing numerous functions essential for the body's metabolism, digestion, and detoxification processes. Some of the conditions and areas of focus in hepatology include:

  • Viral hepatitis: Hepatitis B and C are viral infections that can cause chronic liver inflammation, leading to liver damage, cirrhosis, and even liver cancer.
  • Alcoholic liver disease: Excessive alcohol consumption over time can result in liver inflammation, fatty liver, alcoholic hepatitis, cirrhosis, and liver failure.
  • Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) and non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH): These conditions are characterized by the accumulation of fat in the liver, which can progress to inflammation, fibrosis, and cirrhosis. NAFLD and NASH are often associated with obesity, diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.
  • Liver cirrhosis: A late-stage scarring of the liver that occurs due to chronic liver diseases, leading to impaired liver function.
  • Liver cancer: Both primary liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma) and secondary liver cancer (metastatic cancer that spreads to the liver) fall under the purview of hepatology.
  • Liver diseases: Various liver conditions, including hepatitis, cirrhosis, fatty liver disease, and liver cancer.
  • Autoimmune liver diseases: Conditions such as autoimmune hepatitis, primary biliary cholangitis (formerly known as primary biliary cirrhosis), and primary sclerosing cholangitis involve the immune system attacking the liver and bile ducts.
  • Genetic liver diseases: Examples include hemochromatosis, Wilson disease, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, and hereditary liver conditions that can lead to liver damage and dysfunction.

    • Hepatologists employ various diagnostic tools and techniques to assess liver health, such as blood tests, imaging studies (e.g., ultrasound, CT scan, MRI), liver biopsy, and non-invasive elastography methods. They may also use advanced procedures like endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) and transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt (TIPS) for therapeutic purposes.
